A guided journey to the Moon

Explore the Moon is an interactive, scroll-driven voyage across 384,400 kilometres of space, ending in lunar orbit around a high-definition globe built from NASA's Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter imagery. Eight legendary regions can be explored in detail.

Tycho — Impact crater

The Moon's youngest great ray crater. Diameter 85 km; depth 4.8 km; estimated age ≈ 108 million years; coordinates 43.31°S · 11.36°W.

Tycho is one of the youngest large craters on the Moon — so recent that its spectacular system of bright rays still streaks across a quarter of the near side, stretching more than 1,500 km. At full moon it is the most conspicuous feature on the entire disc. Its terraced walls drop 4.8 km to a floor crowned by a 2-km-high central peak, formed when shocked rock rebounded seconds after the impact.

Named by Giovanni Riccioli in 1651 after Tycho Brahe (1546–1601), the Danish astronomer whose precise naked-eye observations laid the groundwork for Kepler's laws.

  • Samples of Tycho ejecta collected by Apollo 17, 2,000 km away, date the impact at roughly 108 million years ago.
  • Surveyor 7 — the last of NASA's robotic landers — touched down on Tycho's northern rim in January 1968.
  • Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter imaged ponds of impact melt and a mysterious 120-m boulder perched on the summit of the central peak.

Copernicus — Impact crater

The 'Monarch of the Moon'. Diameter 93 km; depth 3.8 km; estimated age ≈ 800 million years; coordinates 9.62°N · 20.08°W.

Rising from the dark lavas of Oceanus Procellarum, Copernicus is the archetype of a complex impact crater: terraced walls, a chain of central peaks 1.2 km high and a vast apron of bright ejecta. Its formation is used to define the Copernican period, the most recent chapter of lunar geological time. The 19th-century selenographer Thomas Elger called it 'the Monarch of the Moon'.

Named after Nicolaus Copernicus (1473–1543), who placed the Sun at the centre of the solar system. Riccioli — a Jesuit who rejected heliocentrism — quipped that he had 'thrown Copernicus into the Ocean of Storms'.

  • Lunar Orbiter 2's 1966 oblique view of its peaks was hailed as the 'Picture of the Century'.
  • Apollo 12 landed on one of its rays; sample dating suggests the impact occurred about 800 million years ago.
  • Spectra of its central peaks reveal olivine — rock hoisted up from many kilometres beneath the crust.

Plato — Lava-flooded crater

The dark, mirror-smooth 'Great Black Lake'. Diameter 101 km; depth ≈ 1 km rim above floor; estimated age ≈ 3.84 billion years; coordinates 51.60°N · 9.40°W.

Perched between Mare Imbrium and Mare Frigoris, Plato is a walled plain whose floor was flooded by basaltic lava soon after its formation, leaving one of the darkest and smoothest surfaces on the Moon. Its rim casts dramatic, needle-like shadows across the floor at sunrise. For centuries observers have reported fleeting glows and mists here — the famous 'transient lunar phenomena' — none ever confirmed.

Named after the Greek philosopher Plato (c. 428–348 BC). The 17th-century astronomer Johannes Hevelius had earlier called it 'the Greater Black Lake'.

  • Crater counts on its lava floor give an age of about 3.84 billion years, just after the Imbrium basin formed.
  • Its floor reflects only ~6% of sunlight, among the lowest albedos of any lunar surface.
  • A 2-km-wide landslide scar on the western rim records the collapse of its terraced walls.

Clavius — Impact crater

A giant among craters — and home to sunlit water. Diameter 231 km; depth ≈ 3.5 km; estimated age ≈ 4 billion years; coordinates 58.40°S · 14.40°W.

One of the largest craters on the near side, Clavius is old enough that its rim has been softened and overprinted by countless later impacts — a graceful arc of progressively smaller craters curves across its floor, a favourite test for telescopes. Arthur C. Clarke placed the lunar base of '2001: A Space Odyssey' beneath its floor.

Named after Christopher Clavius (1538–1612), the Jesuit mathematician and astronomer who designed the Gregorian calendar we still use today.

  • In 2020 NASA's airborne SOFIA observatory detected molecular water (H₂O) in sunlit soil at Clavius — the first such detection, at ~100–400 ppm.
  • The crater chain on its floor (Rutherfurd, Clavius D, C, N, J...) forms a near-perfect diminishing sequence.
  • Its size is on the boundary between craters and impact basins, dating from around 4 billion years ago.

Mare Imbrium — Lunar mare · impact basin

The Sea of Showers — scar of a colossal impact. Diameter 1,145 km; depth Basin ring ~7 km relief; estimated age Basin ≈ 3.9 Ga · lavas 3.3–3.7 Ga; coordinates 32.80°N · 15.60°W.

Mare Imbrium fills the second-largest impact basin on the Moon, blasted out ~3.9 billion years ago by a protoplanet-scale body and later flooded by successive tides of basaltic lava. The impact was so violent that grooves radiating from it — the Imbrium Sculpture — scar terrain hundreds of kilometres away, and it may mark the peak of the Late Heavy Bombardment.

Latin for 'Sea of Showers' or 'Sea of Rains' — early astronomers believed the dark plains were water. The name was formalised by Riccioli in 1651.

  • A 2016 study of the Imbrium Sculpture estimated the impactor at ~250 km across — the size of a small protoplanet.
  • Apollo 15 landed on its south-eastern edge at Hadley Rille in 1971, beside the Apennine mountain ring.
  • Soviet Lunokhod 1 (1970) and China's Chang'e 3 with the Yutu rover (2013) both drove across its lavas.

Mare Tranquillitatis — Lunar mare

The Sea of Tranquility — where humans first landed. Diameter 873 km; depth Basalt fill up to ~1 km; estimated age Lavas ≈ 3.6–3.9 billion years; coordinates 8.50°N · 31.40°E.

On 20 July 1969, Apollo 11's lunar module Eagle settled onto the southwestern corner of this ancient lava plain, and humanity walked on another world for the first time. The mare's basalts are unusually rich in titanium, giving it a subtle steel-blue tint visible even in binoculars.

Latin for 'Sea of Tranquility', named by Riccioli in 1651. Its calm-sounding name made history when Neil Armstrong radioed: 'Houston, Tranquility Base here. The Eagle has landed.'

  • Tranquility Base — Apollo 11, 20 July 1969: the first human footprints on another world.
  • Ranger 8 (1965) and Surveyor 5 (1967) surveyed the mare before the crewed landing.
  • Its titanium-rich basalts (ilmenite) are a prime candidate ore for future oxygen extraction.
  • Recently discovered pits here — the Mare Tranquillitatis Pit — may open into lava-tube caves suitable for habitats.

Oceanus Procellarum — Lunar mare — the only 'ocean'

The Ocean of Storms — largest dark plain on the Moon. Diameter ≈ 2,568 km across; depth Basalt fill hundreds of metres; estimated age Lavas from 3.7 down to ≈ 2.0 billion years; coordinates 18.40°N · 57.40°W.

Sprawling over 10% of the entire lunar surface, Procellarum is the Moon's great western dark plain. It sits atop the Procellarum KREEP Terrane, a region anomalously rich in radioactive elements whose heat kept volcanism alive here for well over a billion years longer than anywhere else — producing the youngest lavas ever sampled on the Moon.

Latin for 'Ocean of Storms' — the only mare grand enough to be titled an ocean. Renaissance astronomers blamed it for bad weather when it faced Earth.

  • Luna 9 achieved the first-ever soft landing on another world here in February 1966.
  • Apollo 12 (1969) made the first pinpoint landing, touching down 163 m from Surveyor 3.
  • China's Chang'e 5 (2020) returned basalts only ~2.0 billion years old — the youngest Moon rocks ever collected.
  • GRAIL gravity data revealed a giant rectangle of buried rift valleys beneath it, hinting the 'basin' is volcanic-tectonic, not an impact scar.

Shackleton — South-polar crater

Eternal night below, eternal light above — Artemis country. Diameter 21 km; depth 4.2 km; estimated age ≈ 3.6 billion years; coordinates 89.90°S · 0.00°E.

Shackleton sits almost exactly at the lunar south pole. Sunlight grazes its rim at near-zero elevation forever, so ridges nearby are 'peaks of eternal light' bathed in sun for over 80% of the year — while the crater floor has not seen the Sun for billions of years and idles near −180 °C, among the coldest places in the solar system. That combination of constant power and trapped ice makes this the most coveted real estate on the Moon.

Named after Sir Ernest Shackleton (1874–1922), the Antarctic explorer — fittingly, for a crater at the Moon's own south pole.

  • Neutron and radar data from Lunar Prospector, Chandrayaan-1 and LRO point to water ice trapped in its permanently shadowed floor.
  • ShadowCam aboard Danuri returned the first detailed images of its pitch-dark interior in 2023.
  • The Shackleton rim region is a leading candidate landing zone for NASA's Artemis crewed missions.

Lunar landing sites

The experience also maps fourteen historic landing sites, from the first Soviet Luna probes and the six crewed Apollo landings to the modern Chang'e and Chandrayaan-3 missions.

Apollo 11 — Crewed landing

The first humans on another world. Operated by NASA · United States; landed 20 July 1969 in Mare Tranquillitatis at 0.67°N · 23.47°E; crew: Neil Armstrong · Buzz Aldrin.

Tranquility Base. At 20:17 UTC on 20 July 1969 the lunar module Eagle touched down with seconds of fuel to spare, and six hours later Neil Armstrong stepped onto the surface — 'one small step for man, one giant leap for mankind.' Armstrong and Aldrin spent 21 hours on the Moon and carried home 21.5 kg of rock and soil.

  • The first crewed landing on another celestial body.
  • First samples ever returned from another world.
  • Left a seismometer and a laser retroreflector still ranged from Earth today.

Apollo 12 — Crewed landing

A pinpoint landing beside Surveyor 3. Operated by NASA · United States; landed 19 November 1969 in Oceanus Procellarum at 3.01°S · 23.42°W; crew: Pete Conrad · Alan Bean.

Apollo 12 proved that precision landing was possible, setting down just 163 m from the Surveyor 3 probe that had arrived two years earlier. Conrad and Bean walked over to the old lander, photographed it and cut away pieces to bring home — a first study of how the lunar environment ages hardware.

  • First precision landing on the Moon.
  • Retrieved parts of Surveyor 3 after 31 months of lunar exposure.
  • Deployed the first full ALSEP nuclear-powered science station.

Apollo 14 — Crewed landing

Reaching the Fra Mauro highlands. Operated by NASA · United States; landed 5 February 1971 in Fra Mauro Highlands at 3.65°S · 17.47°W; crew: Alan Shepard · Edgar Mitchell.

Taking over the target abandoned by the aborted Apollo 13, the crew explored the Fra Mauro formation — ejecta thrown out by the giant Imbrium impact. Alan Shepard, America's first astronaut, famously hit two golf balls across the surface before the crew hauled a tool cart toward Cone crater.

  • Sampled ejecta from the colossal Imbrium basin impact.
  • First use of a wheeled equipment transporter on the Moon.
  • Alan Shepard became the only Mercury astronaut to walk on the Moon.

Apollo 15 — Crewed landing

The first mission with a lunar rover. Operated by NASA · United States; landed 30 July 1971 in Hadley–Apennine at 26.13°N · 3.63°E; crew: David Scott · James Irwin.

The first of the science-focused 'J-missions', Apollo 15 landed between the towering Apennine mountains and the winding Hadley Rille. Scott and Irwin drove the first Lunar Roving Vehicle and recovered the 'Genesis Rock', an anorthosite fragment of the Moon's primordial crust.

  • First use of the Lunar Roving Vehicle, greatly extending exploration range.
  • Collected the ~4-billion-year-old 'Genesis Rock'.
  • Scott's televised hammer-and-feather drop confirmed Galileo's law of falling bodies.

Apollo 16 — Crewed landing

Into the lunar highlands. Operated by NASA · United States; landed 21 April 1972 in Descartes Highlands at 8.97°S · 15.50°E; crew: John Young · Charles Duke.

Apollo 16 was the only mission to land in the central lunar highlands. Expecting volcanic rock, the crew found terrain built almost entirely of impact breccias — overturning ideas about how the highlands formed. Young and Duke drove the rover to the rim of North Ray crater.

  • First and only landing in the lunar highlands.
  • Disproved the theory that the bright highlands were volcanic.
  • Set an unofficial lunar speed record of ~18 km/h in the rover.

Apollo 17 — Crewed landing

The last time humans walked on the Moon. Operated by NASA · United States; landed 11 December 1972 in Taurus–Littrow at 20.19°N · 30.77°E; crew: Gene Cernan · Harrison Schmitt.

The final Apollo landing carried Harrison Schmitt, the only professional geologist to reach the Moon. In the Taurus–Littrow valley the crew discovered orange soil — tiny glass beads from an ancient volcanic fire-fountain. Gene Cernan remains the last person to have stood on the Moon.

  • Longest Apollo stay: three days and 22 hours of moonwalks.
  • Discovered orange volcanic glass, evidence of explosive lunar volcanism.
  • Returned 111 kg of samples — the largest Apollo haul.

Luna 2 — Impact probe

The first spacecraft to reach the Moon. Operated by OKB-1 · Soviet Union; landed 13 September 1959 in Palus Putredinis at 29.10°N · 0.00°E.

Luna 2 became the first human-made object to touch another celestial body, striking the surface east of Mare Serenitatis at some 3 km/s. Before impact it confirmed that the Moon has no appreciable magnetic field or radiation belt, and scattered Soviet pennants across the ground.

  • First human-made object to reach the Moon.
  • Confirmed the Moon has no global magnetic field.
  • Opened the era of direct lunar exploration.

Luna 9 — Robotic soft-lander

The first soft landing on the Moon. Operated by OKB-1 · Soviet Union; landed 3 February 1966 in Oceanus Procellarum at 7.08°N · 64.37°W.

Luna 9 achieved the first survivable soft landing on another world, airbag-cushioned onto the Ocean of Storms. Its panoramic pictures proved the surface was firm enough to bear a spacecraft's weight, settling long-held fears that landers would simply sink into deep dust.

  • First soft landing on the Moon.
  • First photographs taken from the lunar surface.
  • Proved the regolith could support a landed vehicle — clearing the way for Apollo.

Surveyor 1 — Robotic soft-lander

America's first soft landing. Operated by NASA · United States; landed 2 June 1966 in Oceanus Procellarum at 2.47°S · 43.34°W.

Just four months after Luna 9, Surveyor 1 set down flawlessly and returned more than 11,000 images. The Surveyor programme characterised the surface's bearing strength and composition, directly scouting the ground for the Apollo landings that would follow.

  • First US soft landing on the Moon.
  • Returned over 11,000 surface images.
  • Measured the soil's load-bearing strength for Apollo planning.

Luna 16 — Robotic sample return

The first robotic sample return. Operated by Soviet Union; landed 20 September 1970 in Mare Fecunditatis at 0.67°S · 56.30°E.

Luna 16 drilled into the Sea of Fertility, sealed 101 grams of soil into a return capsule and rocketed it back to Earth — the first fully automated sample return from another world, and proof that robots could bring the Moon home without a crew aboard.

  • First robotic sample return from the Moon.
  • First fully automated round trip to another world.
  • Sampled a different mare than Apollo, broadening lunar geology.

Luna 17 · Lunokhod 1 — Robotic rover

The first wheeled rover on another world. Operated by Soviet Union; landed 17 November 1970 in Mare Imbrium at 38.28°N · 35.00°W.

Luna 17 delivered Lunokhod 1, the first roving remote-controlled vehicle to operate on another world. Driven by a five-person team back on Earth, the bathtub-shaped rover trundled more than 10 km across the Sea of Rains during ten months of operation.

  • First rover to operate on another celestial body.
  • Drove more than 10 km over ten lunar days.
  • Its retroreflector is still ranged by lasers from Earth today.

Chang'e 4 · Yutu-2 — Lander + rover

The first landing on the lunar far side. Operated by CNSA · China; landed 3 January 2019 in Von Kármán crater — far side at 45.44°S · 177.60°E.

Chang'e 4 achieved what no mission had before: a soft landing on the Moon's far side, inside Von Kármán crater within the vast South Pole–Aitken basin. Relayed to Earth by the dedicated Queqiao satellite, its Yutu-2 rover became the longest-lived lunar rover in history.

  • First soft landing on the far side of the Moon.
  • Required a dedicated relay satellite stationed beyond the Moon.
  • Yutu-2 is the longest-operating lunar rover ever built.

Chang'e 5 — Robotic sample return

The youngest Moon rocks ever returned. Operated by CNSA · China; landed 1 December 2020 in Mons Rümker · Oceanus Procellarum at 43.06°N · 51.92°W.

Chang'e 5 brought back 1.7 kg of lunar soil — the first samples returned in 44 years. Crucially, its basalts erupted only about 2 billion years ago, a billion years later than any Apollo sample, proving the Moon stayed volcanically active far longer than anyone had thought.

  • First lunar sample return since 1976.
  • Collected the youngest volcanic rocks yet found on the Moon.
  • Rewrote the timeline of lunar volcanism.

Chandrayaan-3 · Vikram — Lander + rover

The closest landing yet to the south pole. Operated by ISRO · India; landed 23 August 2023 in Near the south pole at 69.37°S · 32.32°E.

Chandrayaan-3's Vikram lander set down farther south than any mission before it, making India the fourth nation to soft-land on the Moon and the first to reach the resource-rich south-polar region. Its Pragyan rover confirmed sulphur and other elements directly in the polar soil.

  • Southernmost lunar landing to date.
  • Made India the fourth country to soft-land on the Moon.
  • Detected sulphur in situ, informing the search for polar resources.
